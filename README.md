## Abstract: 

Large, recurrent copy number variants (CNVs) are among the strongest risk factors for neuropsychiatric conditions, contributing to multiple phenotypes with overlapping psychiatric and cognitive symptoms. However, the cellular effects of rare CNVs in human tissue and the basis of their convergent risk remain unknown. Using single nucleus RNA-sequencing we evaluated the transcriptome in human brain from carriers of nine high-risk neuropsychiatric CNVs and matched non-carriers. Brain tissue from carriers displayed widespread disruptions of gene expression, with thousands of differentially expressed genes, predominately located outside of the respective CNV regions. Our analyses revealed both convergent and divergent transcriptomic impacts across CNVs, although the study was better powered to detect convergent signals that represent impacts present across multiple individuals. Gene expression changes were greater within brains carrying a deletion than in brains carrying a duplication. For mirror CNVs, the direction of effects was often reversed between deletions and corresponding duplications and correlated with CNV gene dosage, suggesting potential cellular consequences of altered dosage that warrants further investigation. Functional enrichment analyses identified a core set of transcriptional changes in genes involved in mitochondrial energy metabolism and synaptic function that converged across CNVs. These findings provide an initial view into shared and non-shared neurobiological impacts of high-risk CNVs in the human brain and indicate promising avenues for future mechanistic and cellular studies.
